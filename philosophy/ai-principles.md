AI is part of the landscape now. It's in our tools, our workflows, and increasingly in the products we create. At PhilaCon Valley, we don't shy away from it — but we don't center our identity on it either. AI is one of many things our members build with, think about, and learn from. It's part of the conversation, not the whole conversation.

These principles guide how we — both as individual builders and as a community organization — approach AI with intention, honesty, and care.

---

## Our Principles

### AI is a tool, not a replacement for craft.

We use AI to move faster, explore ideas, and remove friction. But the craft — the thinking, the decision-making, the care for the person on the other side of what we build — that stays human. AI amplifies the builder. It doesn't replace them.

Think of it like power tools in a woodshop. They let you cut faster and more precisely, but the design, the material choices, the sense of whether something is beautiful and functional — that's still the craftsperson's job.

### We experiment openly and share what we learn.

Our community grows when knowledge moves freely. If you've found a workflow that works, a prompt that unlocks something, or a limitation you've run up against — share it. We learn together by building in public, including how we use AI.

This goes both ways. Sharing what works is valuable. Sharing what doesn't work — where AI fell short, produced biased output, or wasted your time — is equally valuable. Honest accounts of failure teach the community more than polished success stories.

### We think critically about the systems we use and build.

AI tools are not neutral. They reflect the data they were trained on, the priorities of the companies that built them, and the decisions made about who they were designed to serve. We stay curious and critical — asking who benefits, who might be harmed, and whether the tool fits the moment.

We know this firsthand. Many AI systems have well-documented gaps in how they serve Black, Brown, and marginalized communities — from biased facial recognition to language models that default to perspectives that don't represent us. That's not a reason to avoid AI. It's a reason to engage with it more critically, not less.

### We center people, not automation.

When we build with AI, we ask: does this make life better for real people? Does it create opportunity or foreclose it? The communities we come from have often been the last to benefit and the first to be displaced by technology. We build with that history in mind.

Automation for its own sake isn't a value here. If an AI-powered feature removes a job without creating a better alternative, that's not progress — that's extraction wearing a tech label. We build things that open doors, not close them.

### We stay humble about what we don't know.

The AI landscape changes fast. What's true today may not be true in six months. We hold our opinions about specific tools and techniques loosely — and prioritize understanding the fundamentals well enough to adapt.

This means we don't worship any single tool or company. Today's breakthrough is tomorrow's baseline. What lasts is the ability to learn, evaluate, and apply new tools thoughtfully — and that's a skill this community is built to develop.

## Transparency and AI Use

### For members

If you use AI to help create something you share in the community — a blog post, a presentation, code for a hackathon, a resource guide — be upfront about it. Honesty about process is how we all learn faster.

**Say what you used and how you used it.** A simple note like "I used Claude to help draft this and then edited it heavily" or "the initial layout was generated with AI but I reworked the design" is enough. The point isn't to confess — it's to share context that helps others learn from your process.

**Don't pass off AI-generated work as entirely your own.** If someone asks how you built something and the honest answer involves AI, say so. Misrepresenting the process undermines the trust this community is built on.

**The work still has to be yours.** Using AI as a collaborator is different from handing it the keys. If you're sharing code you generated but don't understand, a blog post you didn't meaningfully edit, or a design you can't explain — that's not building. That's copying. The value is in what you learn and create through the process, not in the output alone.

### For the community itself

PhilaCon Valley uses AI in its own operations — communications, event planning, content drafting, and parts of this handbook. When we do, the final product is always reviewed and shaped by a human organizer. AI helps us move faster; it doesn't speak for us. The ideas, decisions, and voice come from the organizer team and the community.

We hold ourselves to the same standard we ask of members. If you ever want to know whether something was AI-assisted, ask. We'll tell you.
